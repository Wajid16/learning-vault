# Sentence and Paragraph Building Blocks

**Course:** Scientific Writing · **Video:** [Part 1](https://youtu.be/klkmWfBbeVw?si=fDAFN-hqUatCh1f2) · **Watched:** 2026-07-06

## TL;DR
Good scientific writing is built from three "bricks": precise word choice, four sentence structures, and a paragraph shaped like a burger (topic → support → conclusion) — mechanics that make dense material easy to follow instead of a wall of text.

## Core Concepts
- **Word choice** — words should be precise, simple (don't force the reader to look something up), and necessary; omit needless words. Counter-example given: absurdly padded, jargon-heavy rewrites of a plain fact (written just to dodge plagiarism detectors) — presented as exactly what *not* to do.
- **Four sentence types**:
  - **Simple** — one independent clause. *"Biryani is delicious."*
  - **Compound** — two or more independent clauses joined by a **FANBOYS** conjunction (For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So) or a semicolon.
  - **Complex** — one independent clause + at least one dependent clause, joined by a subordinator (because, although, if, when, since, while).
  - **Compound-complex** — two or more independent clauses + at least one dependent clause.
  - Scientific writing leans on FANBOYS conjunctions, with an occasional semicolon sentence to show range. Caution flagged in the video itself: don't trust random example sentences pulled off the internet without checking them — one "compound-complex" example the instructor found online was actually grammatically wrong.
- **Paragraph = the burger model**:
  - Top bun = **topic sentence** — states what the paragraph is about.
  - Filling (the biggest part) = **supporting sentences** — evidence/reasons, often signposted First/Second/Finally.
  - Bottom bun = **concluding sentence**.
  - Worked example: "Canada is one of the best countries in the world" → supporting reasons (healthcare, education, clean cities) → "As a result, Canada is a desirable place to live."
- **Continuity inside a paragraph**: repeat the key term (or a synonym/pronoun), use transition words, keep a consistent order and point of view, use parallel structure for parallel ideas.
- **Transition word categories** (with examples): Emphasis (undoubtedly, clearly, particularly), Addition (moreover, furthermore, as well as), Contrast (unlike, on the other hand, in contrast), Beginning (first, to start, likewise), Middle (in fact, along with, correspondingly), End (in brief, to conclude, hence, on the whole).
- **The "6 eggs, don't break them" rule** for making an important idea stand out:
  1. Condense/omit less important information.
  2. Subordinate less important information (push it later in the sentence).
  3. Put the important point in the **power position** — the start of the sentence/paragraph.
  4. Label it with an emphasis transition word.
  5. Repeat it, reworded, elsewhere.
  6. **State** it outright — don't just imply it.
