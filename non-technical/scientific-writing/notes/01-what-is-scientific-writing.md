# What Is Scientific Writing?

**Course:** Scientific Writing · **Video:** [Part 1](https://youtu.be/klkmWfBbeVw?si=fDAFN-hqUatCh1f2) · **Watched:** 2026-07-06

## TL;DR
Scientific writing is technical writing that conveys research findings, theories, and methods to other scientists and the public — clearly, concisely, and backed by evidence and prior literature, not by narrative flair. It has a fairly fixed structure and exists in both oral and written form.

## Core Concepts
- **Definition** — a form of *technical* writing (as opposed to creative writing like poetry or novels) that conveys scientific information to other scientists, researchers, and the public.
- **Purpose** — "to communicate research findings, theories, and methodologies in a clear, concise, and systematic manner." It leans heavily on **empirical evidence** (data/quantities used as proof) and references to prior research (= **literature**) to support claims.
- **The cooking analogy** — deciding to cook a dish (bhindi/okra) the whole family will like maps directly onto the scientific method: forming the **idea** (something everyone likes) → gathering input from father, mother, siblings (= a **literature review**) → actually cooking it, making real choices along the way (the **experiment**) → asking everyone afterward how it tasted (the **results**). Writing this whole process down *is* scientific writing. If the neighborhood already happily eats bhindi, that's literature-based reasoning supporting your choice before you even start.
- **Typical structure** — Title → Abstract → Introduction (or Literature Review) → Methods/Methodology → Results → Discussion → References. The middle sections shuffle by document type (Methods can sit before or after Results/Discussion), but References are always last.
- **Two modes of scientific communication**:
  - **Oral** — seminars, oral presentations, 2–3 minute "science pitch" talks, researcher nights, science fairs, poster sessions, conference talks.
  - **Written** — research papers, review articles, theses, meta-analyses, project proposals, and "rapid communication" (tweets/X posts, LinkedIn posts, YouTube/reels).
  - Key point: oral communication is *built on* written communication — you write first, then speak from what you wrote.
- **Who actually needs this skill**: students (to earn a degree), researchers (to publish results), scientists (to secure funding), and content writers/bloggers/podcasters (to translate science for the public).
