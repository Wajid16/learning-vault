# Why Publish, and How Impact Gets Measured

**Course:** Scientific Writing · **Video:** [Part 1](https://youtu.be/klkmWfBbeVw?si=fDAFN-hqUatCh1f2) · **Watched:** 2026-07-06

## TL;DR
Doing the research isn't the finish line — until it's published, it hasn't really counted. Once published, its reach and credibility get judged by a handful of standard metrics (citations, h-index, i10-index, journal impact factor), and by how well the result was *told*, not just how good it was.

## Core Concepts
- Core one-liner: **"If research is not published, it means it was not really research."** Work that stays in a drawer or on a university shelf is, functionally, work that didn't happen.
- Cited fact: **95% of theses/research stays on university shelves; only 5% reaches industry or real-world application.**
- **Storytelling matters almost as much as substance** — a mediocre result presented well can land better than a strong result presented badly. Example given: ChatGPT 3.5 was hyped on release despite hallucinating, giving wrong answers, and inventing references — the positives were pushed to the front and the negatives minimized.
- **Historical argument for publishing**: had Ibn Sina not written the *Canon of Medicine*, had Einstein not published E=mc², had Newton kept his gravity insight to himself, had the COVID-19 vaccine trial data or OpenAI's model stayed private — none of those fields could have advanced the way they did. Publishing is literally how knowledge compounds across people.
- Two practical motivations beyond "sharing knowledge": **claiming priority** (being recognized as first in a finding brings future funding and reputation), and competitive pressure at the national/corporate level (e.g. China vs. USA in tech races, Google/Meta/Apple's innovation cycles) — all of it downstream of published research.
- **Impact metrics**:
  - **Citations** — how many times a paper is referenced by others.
  - **h-index** — reflects output *and* citation strength together: an h-index of 20 means 20 papers each cited at least 20 times.
  - **i10-index** — the count of papers with at least 10 citations each.
  - **Journal Impact Factor** — roughly, how much a journal's papers get read/cited — analogous to a TV channel's TRP ratings or a YouTube channel's subscriber count.
  - Real-world/social impact can outweigh raw citation count — e.g. COVID vaccine researchers had comparatively modest personal citation counts, but their work's societal and commercial reach was enormous.
- Practical step: build a real, searchable presence on **Google Scholar, ResearchGate, and ORCID** — the profiles funders and collaborators actually check.
