# Research Paper vs. Review Paper

**Course:** Scientific Writing · **Video:** [Part 1](https://youtu.be/klkmWfBbeVw?si=fDAFN-hqUatCh1f2) · **Watched:** 2026-07-06

## TL;DR
A research paper reports your own original study using primary data; a review paper critically analyzes existing literature to map what's already known — and, crucially, to surface the gap you should actually research next.

## Core Concepts
- **The cooking analogy, reused**: cooking your own pakoras and reporting the recipe and results = a **research paper** (primary data — you generated it). Reading about how *other* people made pakoras and comparing/reporting their write-ups, without cooking anything yourself = a **review paper** (secondary data — someone else generated it).
- **Five differences**:
  1. **Purpose** — research = an original, primary-literature study of a specific question; review = a critical analysis of previously published literature, secondary literature.
  2. **Data** — research uses **primary data** (self-collected via experiments/surveys); review relies mainly on **secondary data** (though a review can include some primary analysis, e.g. a meta-analysis run on existing datasets).
  3. **Process** — research: formulate a question → collect raw data → analyze it → describe the methodology in enough detail to be replicable. Review: pick a topic → summarize existing literature → give newcomers a fast "what's happened in the last 3/5/10 years" overview; usually written by someone already experienced in the field.
  4. **Content** — research includes hypothesis, methodology, results, interpretation, and suggestions for future research. Review compares studies for agreement/discrepancy like a judge, and specifically names the **study gap** — the thing nobody's actually answered yet.
  5. **Length** — both typically run 3,000–6,000 words excluding references (research occasionally up to 12,000 for an exceptionally strong paper; some journals ask for 2,000–2,500 "mini reviews" or ~3-page "letters to the editor").
- **Practical recommendation**: write at least 2 review articles in your first semester of a Master's or PhD, *before* starting your own experiments — that's the concrete way to find the real gap instead of accidentally repeating decades-old work.
