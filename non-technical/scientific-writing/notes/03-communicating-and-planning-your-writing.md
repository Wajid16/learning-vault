# Communicating to Your Audience, and the 5-Step Writing Process

**Course:** Scientific Writing · **Video:** [Part 1](https://youtu.be/klkmWfBbeVw?si=fDAFN-hqUatCh1f2) · **Watched:** 2026-07-06

## TL;DR
Scientific writing exists to deliver a message to a specific audience — often taxpayer-funded research, which creates a duty to be understood. That intent (simple, clear, well-communicated) gets turned into an actual paper through a five-step process: plan, write, revise, format, finalize.

## Core Concepts
- **Why communicate at all**: publicly funded research is usually paid for by taxpayers, who are the real stakeholders — scientists owe them a comprehensible account of what was done, not just a filed report.
- **Three rules for the message itself**:
  - **Simplicity** — plain language reaches both expert and lay readers; the broader your intended audience, the simpler the writing needs to be.
  - **Clarity** — not so simplified it sounds silly, but never rambling or hard to parse — the reader should never be bored or lost.
  - **Good communication** — use more than text: figures, plots, and a **graphical abstract** (one image summarizing the whole paper). Further escalation — video abstracts, translated versions, social posts, podcasts — genuinely increases reach; most scientists stop at "I published it" and skip all of this.
  - Communication is only *complete* once a receiver actually perceives the message — a paper that's published but never read hasn't finished the job.
- **The 5-step writing process**:
  1. **Planning** — reportedly 50–70% of the real work. Clarify your purpose (why you're writing this — degree, publication, newsletter), know your audience (simple for students, technical for expert peers who already know terms like impact factor or h-index), and outline the storyline (sticky notes/whiteboard) *before* writing a word — then get feedback on the outline itself.
  2. **Writing** — follow the outline paragraph by paragraph. Write to communicate the science, not to show off vocabulary. Keep one visible "red thread" running through the whole piece. Prefer active over passive voice. Vary sentence length/structure so it doesn't read as monotone.
  3. **Revising** — work in Pomodoro blocks (25 min focused, phone silenced) to actually catch mistakes; best done early morning or after clearing your head at night. Tools like QuillBot/PaperPal are for catching grammar/typos only — **never** for generating the actual ideas or content. Get feedback from a genuinely critical mentor or co-author, not from someone who'll only praise you, and not from an AI. Expect 5–15 revision passes, each focused on one aspect (structure, then language, then clarity). Reading the draft aloud (or recording yourself reading it) catches awkward phrasing your eyes skip over.
  4. **Formatting** — only once content is solid: consistent headings/font/spacing, figures/tables placed immediately after the text that references them, matching the target journal's template.
  5. **Finalizing** — proofread as if you were an outside reviewer (your own mistakes are hard to see — enlist a colleague). A well-presented, even average, result can still succeed with human reviewers who respond to good storytelling.
