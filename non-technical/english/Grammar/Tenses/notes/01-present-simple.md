# Present Simple

**Course:** English → Grammar → Tenses · **Video:** [Learn all the Tenses in English: Complete Course](https://youtu.be/O9S70oJAivI) (Rebecca Ezekiel, engVid) — segment ~00:10:20–00:46:23 · **Watched:** 2026-07-06

## TL;DR
Present simple is for what's permanent, general, or regularly true — not for what's happening right now. Form: base verb for I/you/we/they, verb+**s** for he/she/it; negative and question use the helping verb **do/does**.

## The 12-tense map (context from the intro)
English has 12 tenses, split into **6 basic** (learned first) and **6 advanced**, and also into **simple** vs **continuous/progressive** columns. Patterns learned in the basic tenses (grammar, spelling, pronunciation) carry forward into the advanced ones — later tenses aren't uniformly "harder," some parts get *easier* because the pattern is already familiar. Present simple is tense #1 in the series.

## When We Use It
Five situations:
1. **Permanent situations** — "We live in New York." / "He works at a bank." (not literally forever, just not temporary/changing daily)
2. **Routines** — "I wake up at 6:00 every day."
3. **Facts** — "The sun rises in the east." (scientific/general truth, not a personal choice)
4. **Schedules** — "Our class starts at 9:00." / "The flight leaves at noon." (regularly scheduled, timetabled events)
5. **Frequency adverbs** cue this tense: always (100%) → often → sometimes (~50%) → rarely → never (0%). E.g. "She always takes the bus." / "She never takes the bus."

**Nuance from the comparison lesson:** because schedules are regular, present simple can also describe a *scheduled future* event: "The flight leaves at 7:00pm **tomorrow**." The time marker (tomorrow, next week) is what makes it clearly future rather than habitual — without it, "the flight leaves at 7pm" just reads as the standing schedule. See [[02-present-continuous]] for the equivalent future use in that tense.

## Form
| | Positive | Negative | Question |
|---|---|---|---|
| I / You / We / They | work | do not (don't) work | Do I/you/we/they work? |
| He / She / It | work**s** | does not (doesn't) work | Does he/she/it work? |

- Only place in the whole tense that adds **-s** to the verb is the he/she/it **positive**. Negative and question fall back to the base verb ("He doesn't work", "Does he work?").
- Question words (who, what, when, where, why, how, how much, how many, how often) simply go *before* the do/does structure: "**Where** do you work?", "**How often** does she work?" — the do/does + subject + base-verb order never changes.

## Spelling changes (he/she/it, positive only)
- Most verbs: add **-s** → dance→dances, cook→cooks, sleep→sleeps.
- Verb ends in **s, sh, ch, x**: add **-es** → kiss→kisses, wash→washes, teach→teaches, fix→fixes.
- Verb ends in **consonant + y**: drop y, add **-ies** → study→studies, try→tries, fry→fries. (If a *vowel* comes before the y, no change — not covered explicitly in this segment, worth confirming later.)
- Irregular: go→go**es**, do→do**es**, have→**has**.

## Short Answers
Take the helping verb straight from the question:
- "Do they need help?" → **Yes, they do.** / **No, they don't.**
- "Does he speak French?" → **Yes, he does.** / **No, he doesn't.**
- The **positive short answer can never be contracted** ("Yes, they do" — not "Yes, they'd" or similar). Only the negative contracts (don't/doesn't).

## Common Mistakes
1. **Wrong tense** — using present continuous for something permanent: "I am living in Tokyo" is wrong if Tokyo is where you actually live; should be "I live in Tokyo." (Full contrast in [[02-present-continuous]].)
2. **Wrong verb form** — subject-verb agreement: "We likes to travel" → "We like to travel"; "They doesn't eat vegetables" → "They don't eat vegetables"; "Does you talk to him often?" → "Do you talk to him often?"
3. **Spelling** — "She trys to save money" → "She tries..." (consonant+y rule).
4. **Missing helping verb** in a question — "What time you finish work?" → "What time **do** you finish work?" (question word doesn't remove the need for do/does).
