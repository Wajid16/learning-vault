# Present Continuous (Present Progressive)

**Course:** English → Grammar → Tenses · **Video:** [Learn all the Tenses in English: Complete Course](https://youtu.be/O9S70oJAivI) (Rebecca Ezekiel, engVid) — segment ~00:46:23–01:43:22 (includes the Present Simple vs. Present Continuous comparison lesson) · **Watched:** 2026-07-06

## TL;DR
Present continuous is for what's happening right now, happening "around now," or temporary — never for permanent facts, and never with stative verbs. Form: subject + **to be** + verb**-ing**.

## Form
Built on the verb **to be** — get that right and the rest follows:

| | Positive | Negative | Question |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | am working | am not (I'm not) working | Am I working? |
| You/We/They | are working | are not (aren't) working | Are you/we/they working? |
| He/She/It | is working | is not (isn't) working | Is he/she/it working? |

**Contractions:**
- Positive: I'm, you're, we're, they're, he's, she's, it's.
- Negative — two valid forms, both fully correct (not one "more correct" than the other): "you're not" **or** "you aren't"; "he's not" **or** "he isn't". ("I'm not" has only one form.)

## Spelling changes (adding -ing)
- Most verbs: just add **-ing** → eat→eating, walk→walking.
- Verb ends in **e**: drop the e, add -ing → use→using, take→taking.
- Verb ends in **ie**: drop ie, add y, then -ing → lie→ly**ing**, tie→ty**ing**.
- **C-V-C pattern** (consonant-vowel-consonant, reading the verb from the end): double the last consonant → clap→clap**ping**, sit→sit**ting**.

## When We Use It
1. **Happening right now** — "The baby is sleeping." / "It's raining."
2. **Happening around now** (not this exact second, but currently in progress, duration unknown) — "He is writing a book." / "He's working on a project."
3. **Temporary** — "We are staying at a hotel." (vs. permanently living somewhere)
4. **A trend** — something changing/developing ("The prices of homes are increasing") or something currently popular/fashionable ("People are wearing bright colors this summer").
5. *(More advanced)* **Repeated + negative/complaining**, usually with "always" — "They're **always** making noise." Contrast: "always" + present simple ("They **always** help us") is a neutral fact/routine, not a complaint.
6. *(More advanced)* **Future**, but only with an explicit time marker — "She's flying to Mexico **next week**." Without the time marker, "She's flying to Mexico" just means it's happening now. Compare to present simple's scheduled-future use in [[01-present-simple]] — both tenses can express a future event, but continuous needs the marker to disambiguate from "right now."

## When NOT to Use It
- **Permanent actions/activities** — where you live, where you work. "I am living in Canada" is wrong if that's permanent; must be "I live in Canada" (present simple).
- **Stative verbs** — verbs describing a mental/emotional state, possession, or certain senses cannot take the continuous form (true for *all* continuous tenses in English, not just this one). Wrong: "I am understanding English," "We are liking this show," "They are having a car," "Are you needing help?" Correct: "I understand," "We like," "They have," "Do you need help?" `need` and `want` are especially common stative-verb traps.

## Short Answers
Same take-it-from-the-question rule as present simple:
- "Is he working late?" → **Yes, he is.** / **No, he isn't.**
- Positive short answer **cannot be contracted** ("Yes, he's" is wrong) — only the negative contracts.
- When the question is "Are you...?", answer with "I": "Are you studying?" → "Yes, I am." / "No, I'm not."

## Common Mistakes
1. **Missing "to be"** — "My brother watching the news" → "My brother **is** watching the news."; "John not helping" → "John **is not** helping" / "John's not helping" / "John isn't helping."
2. **Missing "to be" in a question** — "She going?" → "**Is** she going?"
3. **Wrong form of "to be"** (agreement) — "Bob and Maria **is** driving home" → "**are** driving home" (Bob and Maria = they). Also: "ain't" is slang, not acceptable in academic/formal English — use "isn't"/"aren't" instead.
4. **Wrong main verb form** — "She is **do** her homework" → "is **doing**"; "The game is **start** now" → "is **starting**."
5. **Spelling** — "lieing"→lying, "siting"→sitting, "writeing"→writing; missing apostrophe: "Were going to the mall" → "**We're** going to the mall."
6. **Stative verb used anyway** — "I am needing some water" → "I need some water"; "I'm wanting to visit Hawaii" → "I want to visit Hawaii."
7. **Used for a permanent situation** — asked "What does your company do?", answering "We are manufacturing cars" is wrong (permanent business activity) → "We **manufacture** cars."

## Common Confusions: Present Simple vs. Present Continuous
Core split: **permanent/general/true-in-general** (simple) vs. **now/temporary/in-progress** (continuous). Same idea, different lens, several pairs from the comparison lesson:

| Present Simple | Present Continuous |
|---|---|
| "I live in this building." (permanent) | "I am staying at a hotel." (temporary) |
| "He plays the guitar." (knows how, in general) | "He is playing the guitar." (doing it right now) |
| "We watch the news at 9pm." (routine) | "We are watching the news." (happening now) |
| "The sun sets in the west." (fact) | "Look, the sun is setting!" (happening now) |
| "The price rises every year." (general pattern) | "The price is rising this year." (a trend, in progress) |
| "She always takes the bus." (neutral routine) | "She is always complaining about the bus." (negative/complaint, advanced use) |
| "The flight leaves at 7pm." / "...at 7pm tomorrow." (schedule, can extend to future) | "The flight is leaving." (right now) / "...at 7pm tomorrow." (future, needs the time marker) |

**Action vs. stative verbs** is the other major axis: action verbs (run, jump, sing, dance, read, write) work in *both* tenses ("She runs" / "She is running"). Stative verbs (know, like, want, need, have-as-possession, understand, etc.) only work in present simple — never in present continuous or any other continuous tense.
